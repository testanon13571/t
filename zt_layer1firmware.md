## Architecture Overview: The Three NIST SP 800-207 Planes

NIST SP 800-207 defines Zero Trust Architecture around a trust arbitration model composed of three logical planes. Every control in this document is assigned to one or more planes.

**Trust Signal Flow:** Endpoint signals (health attestation, EDR telemetry, device compliance) flow up to the Management Plane. The PDP evaluates these signals against policy and issues an access decision. The PEP enforces that decision at every resource access attempt. All access is per-session, per-resource — no standing implicit trust.

## Layer 1: Firmware

**CISA Pillars:** Devices

### Threats Addressed

Firmware-layer threats operate entirely below the OS, evading all userland security controls including EDR, AV, and MDM agents. Key threats include: BIOS/UEFI bootkit implants (MosaicRegressor, CosmicStrand, BlackLotus/CVE-2023-24932), Secure Boot bypass via stale DBX revocation databases, supply chain compromise of firmware during device manufacturing or OEM logistics, TPM/Secure Enclave tampering (CVE-2023-1017/1018 on firmware TPM implementations), persistent UEFI implants surviving full OS reinstallation and disk wipe, and Intel Management Engine / AMD PSP exploitation enabling out-of-band persistent access.

> **Attacker Threat Model:** A firmware-level attacker has already defeated every OS-layer control in this architecture. Their objective is to establish persistence below the attestation layer — producing fraudulent device health signals that appear compliant to the Conditional Access PDP while the endpoint remains compromised. Every control in this layer exists to make the hardware root of trust chain (Boot Guard → TPM PCR → HAS token → Conditional Access PDP) tamper-evident and cryptographically unforgeable.

### ZTA Integration

Layer 1 firmware controls operate at the **foundation of the Zero-Trust device trust chain**, directly implementing the NIST SP 800-207 requirement that every resource access request be evaluated against a continuous, hardware-attested device health signal.

**Trust signal generation (PDP inputs):** FW-01 (Windows HAS attestation) and FW-03 (Apple Secure Boot MDM compliance) produce the primary device health claims evaluated by the Conditional Access Policy Decision Point. These signals are hardware-bound — FW-01 via TPM PCR 7 measurement, FW-03 via Apple Silicon Secure Enclave LocalPolicy seal — making them unforgeable from OS-layer malware. FW-10 (TPM risk tiering) and FW-13 (Apple MDA) extend the trust signal with device-class assurance data, enabling tiered Conditional Access policies that apply proportionately stricter access controls to lower-assurance device tiers. FW-05 (firmware version compliance) and FW-02 (dbx currency) feed MDM compliance attributes that the PDP evaluates continuously.

**Policy Enforcement (PEPs):** FW-11 (TPM PCR sealing) acts as a passive local PEP — it enforces correct boot measurement by making BitLocker and WHfB credentials inaccessible to any boot chain that deviates from the enrolled baseline. FW-18 (firmware rollback prevention) and FW-20 (CSM disable) serve as UEFI-layer PEPs that restrict the attack surface available at the physical access boundary. FW-25 (AMT disable) eliminates an out-of-band PEP bypass that would allow an attacker to access the device below the Conditional Access enforcement plane.

**Policy Administration (PAP):** FW-09 (Boot Guard procurement), FW-14/FW-15 (supply chain controls), FW-17 (update channel integrity), FW-19 (DFCI), and FW-21/FW-22 (UEFI admin passwords) constitute the Policy Administration Plane for the firmware layer — configuring and protecting the controls that generate PDP signals and enforce PEP actions. Without these controls, the trust signals generated by FW-01 and FW-03 are forgeable.

**Continuous Monitoring (Management Plane):** FW-06 (firmware integrity agent), FW-23/FW-24 (UEFI event logging), FW-04 (LocalPolicy monitoring), FW-12 (TPM Clear detection), and FW-16 (out-of-window update detection) fulfil the NIST SP 800-207 §3.5 continuous monitoring requirement for the firmware layer — forwarding telemetry to the SIEM and triggering automated SOC response workflows when the device trust chain shows evidence of tampering.
